Why Reporting Matters

A strong safety culture relies on learning from every incident, no matter how small. Reporting is not about placing blame; it's about gathering information to prevent recurrence. Both incidents (events that caused harm) and near-misses (events that could have caused harm but didn't) must be reported.

What to Include in a Report:

A good incident report is factual and objective. It should include the 5 W's:

  • Who: Who was involved? Who were the witnesses?
  • What: What happened? Describe the sequence of events. What were the consequences?
  • When: What was the exact date and time of the incident?
  • Where: Where exactly did the incident occur? Be specific.
  • Why: Why did it happen? This involves root cause analysis, looking beyond immediate causes to find underlying system failures.

The Value of Near-Miss Reporting:

A near-miss is a free lesson. It provides all the information of an actual incident without the injury or damage. Reporting near-misses allows the organization to identify and fix a problem before it leads to a real accident. A high number of near-miss reports is often a sign of a healthy and proactive safety culture, not a dangerous workplace.
